By 2026, a RAG project has transitioned from an "impressive extra" to a baseline requirement for any Machine Learning or AI Engineer portfolio.

In the current market, hiring managers assume you know how to train a model; what they are looking for now is whether you can ground a model in dynamic, private, or specialized data without hallucinating.

Since you haven't settled on a specific project yet, here are three directions tailored to your unique profile (Physics PhD + OTA Startup interest). I’ve ranked them by how well they "clear the baseline" vs. how much they make you stand out.

Option 1: The "ScholarRec" (Research Focus)
The Pitch: A RAG system specifically for high-energy physics papers (arXiv).

Why it clears the baseline: It proves you can handle complex data (LaTeX, equations, and dense academic PDFs) which standard "out-of-the-box" RAG often fails at.

MLE "Flex": Use Hybrid Search (combining BM25 keyword search with Vector dense search) to ensure it finds specific mathematical terms that embeddings might blur together.

Option 2: The "Travel-Agentic" RAG (Startup Focus)
The Pitch: A retrieval system for your OTA (Online Travel Agency) idea that queries real-time flight policies, visa requirements, and destination "vibe" data.

Why it clears the baseline: It shows Agentic RAG. Instead of just answering a question, the system decides where to look (e.g., "Should I check the refund policy DB or the real-time flight status API?").

MLE "Flex": Implement Self-RAG. Have the model critique its own retrieved context to see if it actually answers the user's travel query before it generates a response.

Option 3: The "Code-Base Interpretability" RAG (Technical Focus)
The Pitch: A RAG tool that indexes a complex physics simulation codebase (like something in SageMath or Cadabra) to help researchers understand the logic flow.

Why it clears the baseline: Large-scale code retrieval is one of the hardest RAG problems in 2026.

MLE "Flex": Show off Semantic Chunking. Instead of cutting code every 500 characters, you chunk it by function or class definition.

What "Clearing the Baseline" actually looks like in 2026:
Regardless of which project you pick, a "baseline" project in 2026 must include these three things to be taken seriously:

Evaluation Pipeline: You can't just say "it works." You need to use a framework like Ragas or DeepEval to show your "Faithfulness" and "Answer Relevancy" scores.

A Vector Database: Show you can use a production-grade store like Pinecone, Qdrant, or Milvus, not just a local FAISS index.

Observability: Include a dashboard (like LangSmith or Arize Phoenix) that shows you're tracking where the retrieval failed or where the model hallucinated.
